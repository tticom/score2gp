# Fundamentals of Musical Notation for the Guitar

This document provides a concise yet comprehensive overview of musical notation, focusing specifically on how it applies to the guitar. The terminology utilises traditional British English musical terms alongside their American equivalents for absolute clarity.

## 1. The Staff and the Guitar's Range

The standard musical staff (or stave) consists of five parallel horizontal lines and the four spaces between them. The guitar reads from the **treble clef** (also known as the G clef). 

It is important to note that the guitar is a transposing instrument; it sounds exactly one octave lower than it is written. This convention prevents the music from requiring an excessive number of ledger lines below the staff.

* **Ledger Lines:** Short, horizontal lines added above or below the staff to accommodate notes that fall outside the standard five lines.
* **The Guitar's Written Range:** * **Lowest Note:** The open low E string (6th string) is written as an E below the middle C. On the staff, this is placed below three ledger lines beneath the main five lines.
    * **Highest Reasonable Note:** While dependent on the specific instrument's fret count, a 24-fret guitar reaches a high E. This is written on the fourth ledger line above the staff. 

## 2. Note Values (Duration)

Notes indicate not only pitch but also duration. The sequence of notes follows a strictly fractional subdivision. The practical upper limit of speed for most guitar music caps at the demisemiquaver.

* **Semibreve (Whole Note):** An open oval. It represents the longest standard duration, typically held for four beats in a 4/4 time signature.
* **Minim (Half Note):** An open oval with a vertical stem. It lasts for exactly half the duration of a semibreve (two beats).
* **Crotchet (Quarter Note):** A solid (filled) oval with a stem. It lasts for one beat.
* **Quaver (Eighth Note):** A solid oval with a stem and one 'tail' or 'flag'. It lasts for half a beat. Multiple quavers are often connected by a single horizontal beam across their stems.
* **Semiquaver (Sixteenth Note):** A solid oval with a stem and two tails. It lasts for a quarter of a beat. Multiple semiquavers are beamed together with two parallel horizontal lines.
* **Demisemiquaver (Thirty-Second Note):** A solid oval with a stem and three tails (or three beams when grouped). It lasts for one-eighth of a beat. This is generally the reasonable upper limit for complex, fast guitar runs (e.g., swift arpeggios or rapid sweep picking).

*(Note: Hemidemisemiquavers, or 64th notes, exist but are exceedingly rare in standard guitar repertoire and border on the impractical for continuous play).*

## 3. Bars (Measures)

The staff is divided horizontally into temporal segments called **bars** (or measures) by vertical lines known as **bar lines**.

* **Function:** Bars organise the music into manageable, regular rhythmic groupings determined by the time signature (e.g., 4/4 time means four crotchet beats per bar).
* **Display:** The notes and rests within a single bar must mathematically add up to the total duration dictated by the time signature.
* **Double Bar Line:** Two vertical lines placed together, typically used to indicate the end of a section or the very end of a piece of music.

## 4. Rests

For every note value, there is a corresponding symbol for silence of the exact same duration, known as a rest.

* **Semibreve Rest (Whole Rest):** A small solid rectangle hanging *below* the fourth line of the staff.
* **Minim Rest (Half Rest):** A small solid rectangle sitting *on top* of the third line of the staff.
* **Crotchet Rest (Quarter Rest):** A squiggly, lightning-bolt-like symbol.
* **Quaver Rest (Eighth Rest):** A symbol resembling the number '7' with a thicker bulb on the top left.
* **Semiquaver Rest (Sixteenth Rest):** Similar to the quaver rest, but with two branches/bulbs.
* **Demisemiquaver Rest (Thirty-Second Rest):** Similar to the previous rests, but with three branches.

## 5. Dotted Notes

A dot placed immediately to the right of a notehead fundamentally alters its mathematical duration.

* **The Rule:** A dot adds exactly 50% (half) of the original note's value to its total duration.
* **Examples:**
    * A **dotted minim** (dotted half note) equals the duration of a minim plus a crotchet (2 beats + 1 beat = 3 beats total).
    * A **dotted crotchet** (dotted quarter note) equals the duration of a crotchet plus a quaver (1 beat + 0.5 beats = 1.5 beats total).
* **Dotted Rests:** The exact same mathematical rule applies to rests; a dotted rest extends the period of silence by 50% of the rest's original value.
